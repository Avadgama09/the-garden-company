Every gardener eventually stares at a yellowing leaf and asks: "What does this plant need?" The frustrating part is that five different deficiencies can all look like "yellow leaves." The good part is that plants follow rules — where the yellowing appears and what pattern it takes tells you exactly what is missing.

I spent my first monsoon season dumping fertiliser on a tomato plant that kept turning yellow at the bottom. Turns out the real problem was that heavy rains had washed out all the nitrogen from my container mix. More fertiliser just kept getting flushed out. Once I fixed the drainage and switched to slow-release neem cake, the new growth came back green within two weeks.

This guide breaks down the most common nutrient deficiencies you will encounter in Indian home gardens, how to read the visual clues your plant is giving you, and the simplest ways to fix each one.

## The Key Diagnostic Trick: Mobile vs Immobile

Before diving into individual nutrients, learn this one concept — it will save you hours of guessing.

Nutrients inside a plant are either **mobile** or **immobile**. Mobile nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium) can move from older leaves to younger leaves when the plant runs short. So their deficiency symptoms show up on **older, lower leaves first** — the plant sacrifices old growth to feed new growth.

Immobile nutrients (calcium, iron, boron, manganese, zinc) get locked in place once the plant uses them. When these run short, the plant cannot borrow from existing leaves. Symptoms appear on **younger, upper leaves and growing tips first**.

This single observation — old leaves or new leaves? — narrows your diagnosis by half before you even look at the specific symptom.

## The Big Three: NPK Deficiencies

### Nitrogen (N) — The Green Maker

**What it does:** Drives leafy green growth, chlorophyll production, and overall vigour.

**Deficiency symptoms:** Older (lower) leaves turn pale green, then uniformly yellow starting from the leaf tip along the central vein. Severe cases cause leaves to drop. The whole plant looks stunted and thin.

**Common cause in Mumbai:** Monsoon rains leach nitrogen out of containers fast. Overwatering has the same effect. Sandy or low-organic-matter soil holds very little nitrogen.

**How to fix it:**
- Quick fix — diluted compost tea or a water-soluble nitrogen fertiliser
- Long-term — mix neem cake (₹80–120/kg) into soil. It releases nitrogen slowly and deters pests
- Add a 2-inch top-dressing of vermicompost (₹40–80/kg) every 2–3 months

### Phosphorus (P) — For Roots and Flowers

**What it does:** Supports root development, flowering, fruiting, and energy transfer within the plant.

**Deficiency symptoms:** Older leaves develop a purplish or reddish tint, especially on undersides and stems. Leaves may darken to a deep bluish-green before discolouring. Growth is stunted — the plant looks small for its age. Flowering and fruiting are delayed.

**Common cause in Mumbai:** Cool winter mornings (January–February) slow phosphorus uptake even when it is present in soil. Very acidic or very alkaline soil locks phosphorus out.

**How to fix it:**
- Bone meal mixed into soil (organic, slow-release)
- Rock phosphate for long-term availability
- Well-composted cow manure

### Potassium (K) — The Health and Disease Shield

**What it does:** Regulates water use, strengthens cell walls, improves disease resistance, and supports fruit quality.

**Deficiency symptoms:** Leaf edges and tips turn yellow first, then brown and crispy — a scorched appearance. The yellowing starts at margins and moves inward. Fruit and flower quality drops. Plants become more vulnerable to disease.

**Common cause in Mumbai:** Leaching from heavy monsoon rain or frequent watering. Sandy or low-organic-matter potting mixes.

**How to fix it:**
- Banana peels — dry, crush, and mix into soil surface
- Wood ash in small amounts (raises pH, so use carefully)
- Potassium sulfate fertiliser for faster correction

## Secondary Nutrients

### Calcium (Ca) — Cell Builder

**Deficiency symptoms:** Affects **new growth** (calcium is immobile). Young leaf tips die back and turn brown. New leaves appear distorted or curled. The classic sign in vegetables is **blossom end rot** — a dark, sunken spot on the bottom of tomatoes or peppers.

**Common cause in Mumbai:** Inconsistent watering during the April–May heat causes calcium transport to fail, even when calcium is in the soil. Calcium moves with water — if water supply is erratic, calcium does not reach the fruit.

**How to fix it:** Crushed eggshells mixed into soil (slow), gypsum (faster). Most importantly, **water consistently** — blossom end rot is more a watering problem than a calcium problem.

### Magnesium (Mg) — Chlorophyll Core

**Deficiency symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis on **older leaves** — the tissue between veins turns yellow while the veins stay green. Leaves may curl upward or develop orange-red tints. Common on tomatoes, potatoes, and roses.

**How to fix it:** Epsom salt solution — 1 tablespoon per 4 litres of water, applied as a soil drench or foliar spray. Use only when you see confirmed symptoms. Overuse causes salt buildup.

## Micronutrients: Small but Critical

### Iron (Fe)

**Symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis on **young leaves** — veins stay dark green, tissue between turns yellow to almost white in severe cases. Very common in alkaline soils.

**Fix:** Iron chelate or ferrous sulfate as a foliar spray. Long-term: lower soil pH if alkaline (common with Mumbai tap water).

### Manganese (Mn)

**Symptoms:** Similar to iron — interveinal chlorosis on young leaves, but with tan or brown speckling between the veins.

**Fix:** Manganese sulfate foliar spray. Best available at pH 5.5–6.5.

### Zinc (Zn)

**Symptoms:** Short internodes (leaves bunched together). Small, narrow leaves. Interveinal chlorosis on new growth.

**Fix:** Zinc sulfate foliar spray.

### Boron (B)

**Symptoms:** Growing tips die or become distorted. Stems feel hollow. Leaves may thicken and become brittle. Root vegetables develop black heart.

**Fix:** Very dilute borax solution — ¼ teaspoon per 4 litres of water, once per season maximum. Excess boron is toxic.

## Mumbai and Indian Home Gardener Notes

**Monsoon is your biggest nutrient enemy.** Weeks of daily rain flush nitrogen and potassium out of containers. Plan to fertilise with vermicompost and neem cake in early June (before monsoon) and again in October (after monsoon). Slow-release organic amendments hold up better than water-soluble chemical fertilisers during this period.

**Mumbai's tap water is slightly alkaline** (pH 7.5–8.0 in many areas, TDS 200–400 ppm). Over months, this raises soil pH and causes iron lockout — the iron is in the soil, but the plant cannot absorb it at high pH. If you see yellowing between veins on new leaves despite regular feeding, pH is likely the culprit. Flush containers monthly and consider using rainwater (naturally slightly acidic) when available.

**Container soil depletes faster than ground soil.** A 12-inch pot holds limited nutrition. Top-dress with vermicompost every 8–10 weeks. Add neem cake once per season. Use liquid seaweed extract (₹150–250 per bottle) as a foliar spray monthly — it provides trace micronutrients that containers run out of quickly.

**Don't confuse nutrient deficiency with disease or overwatering.** Yellow leaves can mean nitrogen deficiency, root rot, overwatering, or even spider mites. Always check the soil moisture and root health before assuming it is a nutrient issue. If roots are brown and mushy, no amount of fertiliser will help — fix the root problem first.

## Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

**Fertilising a sick or overwatered plant.** If your plant is already stressed from root rot, pests, or waterlogging, adding fertiliser makes things worse. Salts accumulate around damaged roots and burn them further. Fix the underlying problem first, then feed lightly once the plant stabilises.

**Using only one type of fertiliser.** Urea gives nitrogen but nothing else. NPK 19-19-19 gives the big three but no micronutrients. Container plants need the full range. Use a combination: vermicompost for NPK + neem cake for nitrogen + occasional seaweed spray for micronutrients.

**Ignoring pH.** You can add all the iron in the world, but if your soil pH is above 7.5, the plant cannot absorb it. Most vegetables and flowers prefer pH 6.0–7.0. Test with a simple soil pH kit (₹300–500 online) at least once a year.

**Over-applying Epsom salt.** Social media has turned Epsom salt into a cure-all. It only provides magnesium and sulfur. Using it when your plant does not need magnesium causes salt buildup and can block calcium uptake — creating a new problem while trying to fix one that did not exist.

**Diagnosing from a single symptom.** Yellow leaves alone can mean six different things. Always combine the visual clue with its location (old vs new leaves), the pattern (uniform vs interveinal vs edges), and the growing context (recent rain, watering habits, soil age).
