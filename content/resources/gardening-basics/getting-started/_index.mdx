# Getting Started with Gardening: Your First 30 Days as a Home Gardener

The first time I decided to start gardening, I spent three weeks researching. I read about soil pH, fertilizer NPK ratios, companion planting charts, and germination temperatures. By the time I felt "ready," I was too intimidated to actually start. I'd convinced myself gardening was complicated science requiring perfect conditions.

Then my neighbor showed me her balcony—eight thriving pots of methi, mint, and chillies she'd started without reading a single article. "I just put plants in pots and watered them when the soil looked dry," she said, shrugging. That moment taught me something important: most of us overthink the start.

Getting started with gardening isn't about knowing everything before you begin. It's about taking one small action today, observing what happens, adjusting tomorrow, and building from there. This guide cuts through the overwhelm and gives you exactly what you need for your first 30 days as a home gardener in India.

## The Reality Check: What Starting Actually Means

Starting gardening doesn't mean creating an Instagram-worthy balcony oasis overnight. It means getting three plants to survive for three weeks while you figure out watering, sunlight, and soil basics.

Your goal for month one: keep plants alive and learn their language. Not perfection, not abundance, not even flowers or fruit yet. Just three living plants that teach you how to read what they need.

### What You Don't Need to Start

You don't need a green thumb (whatever that means). You don't need expensive tools, fancy pots, or a large space. You don't need to understand botany, memorize plant names, or know how to identify nutrient deficiencies.

You definitely don't need to start with ten different plant types because you saw someone on YouTube growing everything from tomatoes to strawberries in their balcony garden.

### What You Actually Need

Curiosity. Five minutes daily to check on plants. Willingness to learn from mistakes. That's genuinely it. Everything else—soil, pots, water, plants—you can acquire for under ₹800 and set up in one afternoon.

### The Beginner's Advantage

You have zero bad habits yet. You haven't developed the overwatering tendency that kills more plants than anything else. You're watching closely because everything is new, which means you'll notice problems early when they're easy to fix.

First-time gardeners who start small and pay attention often succeed faster than people who've been "trying to garden" for years with twenty neglected pots.

## Day 1-3: Observe Your Space (Don't Buy Anything Yet)

This is the most important step most beginners skip. Before you spend a single rupee or bring home any plants, you need to understand your space.

### The Sunlight Audit

Wake up early on a weekend. At 7 AM, stand on your balcony, terrace, windowsill, or wherever you plan to garden. Note which areas are in direct sun. "Direct sun" means sunlight hits the spot without passing through glass, mesh, or shade—you can feel the warmth.

Check again at 10 AM. Which spots are still sunny? Check at 1 PM (usually peak sun). Check at 4 PM. Check at 6 PM before sunset.

Sketch a simple map or take photos at each time. After three days of observation, you'll know:

- **Which areas get 6+ hours direct sun** (full sun spots—best for tomatoes, chillies, most vegetables)
- **Which get 3-5 hours direct sun** (partial sun—good for leafy greens, herbs like coriander and mint)
- **Which get bright light but little direct sun** (shade—suitable for snake plant, pothos, ferns)

### Why This Matters So Much

Ninety percent of beginner failures trace back to light mismatch. People plant tomatoes (need 6+ hours sun) in spots that get 2 hours. The plant stretches, weakens, never flowers, and eventually dies. The beginner concludes they "can't grow tomatoes" when actually they just planted in the wrong spot.

Get sunlight right, and you've solved half of gardening.

### Other Space Considerations

**Water access:** Is there a tap nearby or do you need to carry water? This affects how many pots you start with. Hauling water for twenty pots gets exhausting fast.

**Wind exposure:** High-rise balconies can be windy. Strong wind dries soil faster (more watering needed) and can damage delicate plants. Note if your space is sheltered or exposed.

**Drainage:** If you're on a balcony, where will excess water go? Make sure drainage won't damage floors below or annoy neighbors. You'll need trays under pots or a designated drainage area.

**Space constraints:** Measure available floor space. Don't just eyeball it. A 3×2 foot balcony holds 6-8 medium pots comfortably. Knowing this prevents overbuying.

## Day 4-7: Gather Your Basic Supplies

Now that you know your space, it's time to acquire the essentials. Keep it minimal. You'll add more as you learn what you actually use.

### The Starter Shopping List

**Three containers with drainage holes:**
- 8-10 inch diameter pots work well for most beginner plants
- Plastic pots are light, cheap (₹40-80 each), don't break easily
- Terracotta pots (₹60-150) breathe better but dry faster—need more frequent watering
- Ensure every pot has holes at the bottom—non-negotiable

**Potting mix (about 10-15 kg to fill three pots):**
- Buy ready-made potting mix (₹150-250 for 10kg) OR
- Mix your own: 1 part garden soil + 1 part cocopeat + 1 part vermicompost
- Avoid using random soil from construction sites—it's often compacted clay with debris

**Small bag of compost or vermicompost (2-3 kg):**
- This feeds your plants every few weeks
- Vermicompost costs ₹40-100 for 1kg depending on source
- Well-rotted cow manure works too (often cheaper from local sources)

**Watering can (1-2 liter capacity with shower nozzle):**
- Gentle shower nozzle prevents washing away seeds or damaging delicate plants
- Long spout helps reach pots at the back
- Budget: ₹100-200

**Hand trowel or old spoon:**
- For mixing soil, making planting holes, transplanting
- A sturdy kitchen spoon works fine to start
- Proper trowel costs ₹50-100

**Optional but helpful:**
- Spray bottle (₹50) for misting delicate seedlings
- Gloves (₹80-150) if you prefer not getting hands dirty
- Small rake or fork for loosening soil surface

### Where to Buy in India

**Local nurseries:** Best option. You see soil quality, check pot drainage, ask questions. Most cities have nurseries near vegetable markets or on main roads. Google Maps often lists them.

**Online options:** Ugaoo, TrustBasket, Kraft Seeds, Amazon India, Flipkart. Convenient for bulk soil purchases or if local options are limited. Check reviews for quality.

**Budget hacks:** Use recycled containers (old paint buckets, large plastic bottles cut in half, broken cooler boxes) instead of buying pots. Free. Just drill drainage holes in the bottom. Get garden soil and compost from a local plant vendor rather than bagged products—often better quality and cheaper.

### Total Budget Expectation

- **Minimum spend:** ₹500-600 (recycled containers, locally sourced soil/compost, basic watering can)
- **Comfortable starter budget:** ₹700-1000 (includes proper pots, quality potting mix, tools)

## Day 8-10: Choose Your First Three Plants

Choosing the right plants makes or breaks your first gardening experience. You want plants that grow fast, forgive mistakes, and produce something useful.

### The Perfect Starter Trio Formula

Pick one from each category for guaranteed success:

**Category 1: Fast-Growing Herb**
- **Mint (pudina):** Grows in partial sun, forgives irregular watering, spreads rapidly, ready to harvest in 3-4 weeks
- **Coriander (dhaniya):** 7-10 days to germinate from seeds, ready in 4 weeks, grows in moderate sun
- **Methi (fenugreek):** Fastest of all—5-7 days germination, harvest in 3 weeks, tolerates heat

**Category 2: Resilient Leafy Green**
- **Spinach (palak):** Grows in partial shade, quick (4-5 weeks to harvest), survives beginner neglect
- **Amaranth (chauli):** Heat-tolerant, fast, nutritious, thrives in Indian summers
- **Lettuce:** Cooler season crop (October-February), grows quickly, doesn't need full sun

**Category 3: Cheerful Flower**
- **Marigold:** Blooms reliably, deters pests, needs full sun, bright orange/yellow flowers boost morale
- **Zinnia:** Easy from seeds, continuous blooms, heat-tolerant, colorful
- **Sunflower (dwarf variety):** Fun to watch grow, impressive results, full sun required

### Why This Trio Works

**Herbs and greens** give quick wins—you see growth within days, harvest within weeks. This builds confidence and momentum.

**Flowers** provide visual feedback beyond just leaves. Watching buds form and open is deeply satisfying and keeps you engaged during the learning curve.

**Different needs** teach you plant diversity. Mint likes moisture, marigold tolerates drought, spinach prefers cooler weather. You learn by doing, not just reading.

### Seeds vs Seedlings: What to Start With

For your very first attempt, **buy seedlings** (small plants in plastic cups) from a nursery. Here's why:

Seeds require precise conditions—correct depth, consistent moisture, right temperature. Mess up any of these and nothing sprouts. Beginners often give up after failed germination.

Seedlings skip that delicate phase. You get a 2-4 week head start. The plant is already established. Your job is maintenance, not creation from scratch.

**Cost:** Seedlings cost ₹20-40 each. Seeds cost ₹20-50 per packet but require more skill. For three plants, spending ₹60-120 on seedlings is worth it for first-time success.

**When to try seeds:** Once you've successfully kept 3-5 nursery plants alive for 6-8 weeks, then experiment with growing from seeds. You'll have built the watering discipline and observation skills needed.

### Plants to Avoid Initially

**Don't start with:**
- Tomatoes (need staking, pruning, 3+ months to fruit, pest-prone)
- Chillies (take 3-4 months to fruit, though they're actually easy once established)
- Any plant labeled "advanced" or "challenging" by the nursery
- Exotic vegetables not commonly grown in your region

You'll get to these later. For now, easy wins build the habit.

## Day 11-15: Set Up Your Pots and Plant

You've observed your space, gathered supplies, and chosen your plants. Now comes the fun part: actually planting.

### Preparing the Pots

Check each pot has drainage holes at the bottom. If using recycled containers, drill 4-6 holes using a heated metal rod or drill bit.

Place a piece of broken terracotta, small stones, or coconut coir fiber over the holes. This prevents soil from washing out while allowing water to drain.

Fill the pot about two-thirds full with your potting mix. Don't pack it down hard—leave it loose and fluffy so roots can penetrate easily.

### Transplanting Seedlings

Water the seedling in its current small pot 1-2 hours before transplanting. Moist soil holds together better.

Make a hole in the center of your prepared pot—slightly larger than the seedling's root ball.

Gently tip the seedling pot upside down, supporting the plant stem between your fingers. Tap the bottom until the root ball slides out. Don't yank the plant by its stem.

Place the root ball in the hole at the same depth it was growing (don't bury the stem deeper). Fill around the sides with more potting mix. Firm gently.

Water immediately after planting. This settles soil around roots and eliminates air pockets.

### First Week After Planting

Place newly transplanted seedlings in **shade or filtered light for 2-3 days**. Transplant shock is real—plants need recovery time before facing full sun.

Water daily for the first week, keeping soil consistently moist (not waterlogged). Check by feeling soil with your finger.

After 3 days of shade, gradually move to their permanent sunlight location. Don't rush this—sudden full sun can wilt stressed plants.

### Planting from Seeds (If You Choose This Route)

Fill pot with potting mix to within 1 inch of rim. Moisten the soil before planting.

**Depth rule:** Plant seeds 2-3 times as deep as the seed's diameter. Tiny seeds (lettuce, coriander) barely cover with soil. Large seeds (beans, sunflower) go 1-2 inches deep.

Space seeds according to packet instructions. For herbs in a 10-inch pot, you can plant 3-4 seeds spaced 2 inches apart.

Cover with thin layer of soil or cocopeat. Spray with water—don't pour, which can wash seeds away.

Keep soil consistently moist until germination. Check twice daily. Most seeds germinate in 5-14 days depending on type and temperature.

Cover pot with newspaper or clear plastic to retain moisture, but remove immediately once sprouts appear. Seedlings need light to avoid becoming leggy.

## Day 16-20: Establish Your Daily Garden Routine

Successful gardening is about consistent small actions, not occasional big efforts. These five days are about building habits that make gardening effortless.

### The Morning Check-In (5 minutes)

**Best time:** 7-9 AM before the day heats up

Walk to your pots. Look at each plant. What's changed since yesterday? New leaves? Taller stem? Wilting? Discoloration?

**The finger test:** Stick your index finger 1-2 inches into the soil of each pot. Does it feel:
- Dry? Water needed.
- Moist/damp? Wait until tomorrow.
- Wet/muddy? Skip watering, check again tomorrow.

This test is more reliable than any schedule. Soil moisture depends on temperature, humidity, pot size, plant type—checking directly eliminates guesswork.

**Water when needed:** Pour water slowly at the base of the plant until you see it draining from bottom holes. Don't just sprinkle the surface. Deep watering encourages deep roots.

**Remove any dead leaves:** Yellow or brown leaves touching soil can harbor pests or disease. Pinch them off and discard.

### The Evening Observation (2 minutes)

Check plants again after 5 PM. Are they perky or drooping? Drooping in evening after morning watering often means either overwatering (roots suffocating) or underwatering (didn't water deeply enough).

This twice-daily observation trains your eye. Within a week, you'll recognize "normal" vs "something's wrong" at a glance.

### Building Watering Discipline

**The #1 beginner mistake:** Watering on a fixed schedule regardless of soil moisture. "It's Tuesday so I water" kills more plants than anything else.

Why schedules fail: A cloudy week means slower evaporation. Monsoon rain pre-waters your pots. Different plants use water at different rates. Fixed schedules ignore all this.

**The right approach:** Check soil before every watering. Let the plant and weather tell you when, not the calendar.

**How often will you actually water?** In Mumbai summer, likely daily. In monsoon, maybe twice a week. In winter, every 2-3 days. It varies—that's why checking is essential.

### Week Three Adjustments

By day 20, patterns emerge. You'll notice one pot dries faster (more sun exposure or plant drinks more). One stays wetter longer (less sun or better moisture retention).

Adjust placement if needed. Move the fast-drying pot to slightly less intense sun or switch to a larger pot that holds more soil/water. Move the slow-drying pot to better sun if the plant seems leggy.

This is active gardening—observing and responding, not following rigid rules.

## Week 3-4: Watch, Learn, and Adjust

By week three, your plants have established. Roots are growing, new leaves appear, and you're settling into a rhythm. This is when learning accelerates because you can see cause and effect quickly.

### Reading Plant Signals

**Happy plant indicators:**
- New growth at the tips—fresh leaves emerging
- Upright posture—stems stand firm, leaves face sun
- Deep green color (for leafy plants)
- Steady growth—visibly taller or wider each week

**Unhappy plant signals:**
- **Yellowing lower leaves:** Usually overwatering. Soil stays wet too long, roots can't breathe. Solution: Water less frequently, improve drainage.
- **Drooping despite wet soil:** Root rot from waterlogging. Solution: Stop watering until soil dries significantly. Consider repotting in fresh, well-draining soil.
- **Crispy brown leaf tips:** Underwatering or low humidity. Solution: Water more consistently, consider light misting for humidity-loving plants.
- **Pale, small leaves, leggy growth:** Insufficient light. Solution: Move to brighter location.
- **Spots, holes, sticky residue on leaves:** Pest issues. Solution: Spray with diluted neem oil (1 tablespoon per liter water), check leaf undersides for insects.

### First Feeding

Around day 20-25, give plants their first supplemental feeding. Container soil depletes faster than ground soil.

Add a 1-inch layer of vermicompost or well-rotted cow manure on the soil surface of each pot. Water after adding. This slow-release feeding gives a nutrient boost without risk of burning plants.

Repeat every 3-4 weeks while plants are actively growing.

### First Pruning (If Needed)

If any plant looks leggy (long stems with few leaves), pinch off the growing tip. This forces the plant to branch out, creating bushier growth.

For herbs like mint or basil, pinching back tips encourages more leaf production—exactly what you want.

Use clean hands or scissors. Pinch just above a leaf node (where leaves attach to stem).

### Dealing with First Problems

**Problem:** Mint spreading too aggressively, crowding the pot.
**Solution:** This is normal. Mint grows fast. Harvest liberally—use in tea, cooking, chutney. Aggressive harvesting keeps it in check.

**Problem:** Coriander bolting (flowering prematurely).
**Solution:** Heat triggers bolting. Harvest all usable leaves immediately. Plant fresh seeds in a cooler spot or wait for cooler weather (October-March).

**Problem:** Marigold not flowering yet.
**Solution:** Patience. Marigolds need 6-8 weeks from planting to first blooms. Ensure it gets 6+ hours sun and has been fed.

**Problem:** Seedlings stretched and weak.
**Solution:** Move to brighter light immediately. Leggy seedlings recover slowly but usually survive if given proper light.

## Your First Harvest Moment

This is why you started. The first time you pinch fresh coriander for your meal or pick methi leaves for parathas—that's when gardening clicks from "interesting hobby" to "I'm actually doing this."

### When to Harvest

**Leafy greens and herbs:** Harvest once the plant has 8-10 mature leaves. Don't strip it bare—take outer leaves first, leave inner young leaves to continue growing. This "cut and come again" approach extends harvest for weeks.

**Methi/fenugreek:** Ready in 3 weeks. Cut stems 2 inches above soil level. It may regrow for a second harvest.

**Coriander:** Harvest when 4-6 inches tall, before flowering. Use scissors to cut stems just above soil.

**Spinach:** Pick outer leaves once they're palm-sized. Inner leaves continue growing.

**Mint:** Harvest stems as needed. Pinch back tips to encourage bushiness. Nearly impossible to over-harvest mint.

### How to Harvest Without Harming the Plant

Use clean scissors or pinch with fingernails. Don't rip or tear, which can damage stems and create entry points for disease.

Harvest in the morning after watering but before noon heat. Leaves are most flavorful and turgid then.

Never take more than one-third of the plant at once (except for crops like methi that you harvest entirely). Leaving two-thirds ensures the plant continues photosynthesis and regrowth.

### The Emotional Win

Your first harvest will feel disproportionately significant. It's a few grams of leaves you could buy for ₹5 at the market. But it represents proof—proof you can grow food, proof you learned something new, proof your effort created life.

That feeling hooks people into gardening for life. It's not about the quantity. It's about the connection.

## Common Week One Problems (And Quick Fixes)

### Problem 1: Nothing Sprouted (Seeds)

**Causes:** Seeds too old, planted too deep/shallow, soil too dry or too wet, temperature too hot/cold.

**Quick fix:** Buy fresh seeds from reputable source. Follow depth guidelines. Keep soil consistently moist (not soggy) until germination. Wait 14-21 days before giving up—some seeds are slow.

**Easier solution:** Start with seedlings instead of seeds until you build more experience.

### Problem 2: Seedlings Fell Over or Disappeared

**Cause:** Damping off (fungal disease that attacks seedlings in overly wet conditions) or cut worms eating stems at soil level.

**Prevention:** Don't overwater seedlings. Ensure good air circulation. Water in morning so soil surface dries during the day. For cutworms, place a collar (cut plastic bottle) around seedling stem base.

### Problem 3: Leaves Turning Yellow After a Few Days

**Causes:** 
- Overwatering (most common)
- Transplant shock
- Nitrogen deficiency
- Natural leaf aging (lower leaves yellow and drop as plant grows—normal)

**Fix:** Reduce watering frequency. Let soil dry more between waterings. Add compost if no improvement after a week.

### Problem 4: Wilting Even After Watering

**Causes:**
- Root rot from too much water (roots are damaged and can't absorb)
- Underwatering (soil so dry it's repelling water instead of absorbing)
- Root-bound plant (roots have nowhere to grow)

**Test:** If soil is wet and plant wilts—stop watering, check roots for brown mush. If soil is dry despite watering—water slowly, let it soak in, repeat. If roots circle the pot densely—repot to larger container.

### Problem 5: Bugs Appearing on Leaves

**Common culprits:** Aphids (tiny green/black insects clustering on new growth), whiteflies (tiny white flies under leaves), spider mites (tiny red dots, fine webbing).

**Quick fix:** Spray plant thoroughly with water to dislodge pests. Mix 1 tablespoon neem oil + 1 tablespoon liquid soap in 1 liter water. Spray all leaf surfaces including undersides. Repeat every 4-5 days for three treatments.

**Prevention:** Inspect plants during your daily checks. Catching pests early means easier control.

### Problem 6: Soil Compacted and Hard

**Cause:** Watering without fluffing soil, lack of organic matter.

**Fix:** Use a fork or stick to gently loosen the top 1-2 inches of soil without damaging roots. Add a layer of cocopeat or compost on top. Future waterings will incorporate it.

## What Happens After 30 Days

By day 30, you'll have kept three plants alive for a month. You've watered correctly more days than incorrectly. You've harvested something edible or admired flowers you grew. You understand your space's sunlight. You've developed a daily routine.

You're no longer a complete beginner. You're a novice gardener—there's a difference. You've earned basic competence.

### The Natural Expansion

Most people who succeed with three plants for 30 days naturally want more. This is good—controlled expansion based on success beats starting with too much.

**Add 2-3 more pots** using the same process:
- Observe where you have space and light
- Choose proven beginner-friendly plants
- Set them up properly with good soil and drainage
- Integrate them into your routine

Expand every 4-6 weeks rather than all at once. This sustainable pace prevents overwhelm.

### Graduating to Slightly Harder Plants

Once you've mastered herbs and leafy greens, try:
- Cherry tomatoes (easier than large tomatoes)
- Green chillies (low maintenance, productive)
- Radish (fast, fun to harvest)
- Green beans (climbing varieties use vertical space)

These require more time and attention but build on the basics you've learned.

### What If You Killed All Three Plants?

This happens. Maybe you traveled and they dried out. Maybe monsoon came and drainage was inadequate. Maybe you overwatered from enthusiasm.

**Restart.** You learned what went wrong. The second attempt almost always succeeds because you've eliminated the mistakes that killed round one.

Gardening skill builds through cycles of trial, failure, adjustment, success. Most experienced gardeners have killed dozens of plants. The difference is they kept restarting until they figured it out.

### The Long Game

Gardening becomes easier the longer you do it. By month three, your routine is automatic. By month six, you recognize problems instantly. By year two, plants thrive almost effortlessly because you've internalized the skills.

Your three starter pots can grow into 10, then 20, then a full terrace garden if you want. Or you can keep just five favorite pots forever. Scale matters less than consistency.

## Mumbai and Indian Home Gardener Notes

**Monsoon management:** If starting during June-September, drainage is critical. Elevate pots on bricks so they don't sit in water. Consider waiting until October if you have no covered area—monsoon is harder for beginners despite being good growing weather.

**Summer heat stress:** March-June temperatures in most Indian cities stress plants. Start with heat-tolerant picks like amaranth, okra, chillies. Provide afternoon shade using 30-50% shade cloth. Water twice daily if needed (early morning and evening).

**Winter advantage:** October-March is the easiest window for beginners in most of India. Moderate temperatures, lower pest pressure, slower evaporation, and most vegetables thrive. If you're reading this and it's near winter—start now.

**Balcony wind:** High-rise apartments get strong wind. Stake tall plants (marigolds, sunflowers). Use heavier terracotta pots that won't tip. Group pots together for wind protection.

**Water quality:** Mumbai's tap water is generally fine for plants. If your building water is extremely hard (white crust on pots), let it sit in a bucket overnight before using. The chlorine evaporates and minerals settle.

**Seed packet confusion:** Many Indian seed packets lack clear instructions. When unsure, plant seeds at a depth equal to twice the seed's diameter. Keep moist. Check germination times online if packet doesn't specify.

**Local resources:** Join WhatsApp groups or Facebook communities of gardeners in your city. They'll recommend reliable nurseries, share seasonal tips, and troubleshoot problems specific to your climate.

**Apartment society restrictions:** Check if your building allows terrace gardening before investing in large setups. Balcony gardening is usually unrestricted. Be considerate with drainage—don't let excess water damage neighbors' walls below.

## Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake 1: Starting with Too Many Plants

Beginners often buy 10-15 plants in initial excitement, then struggle to care for them all. Plants get neglected within weeks.

**Solution:** Three plants. Master those. Add more only after consistent success. Ten thriving plants beat twenty dying plants.

### Mistake 2: Choosing Trendy or Pretty Plants Over Suitable Ones

Buying succulents because they're Instagram-popular, despite your humid balcony. Planting roses because you love flowers, despite being a beginner. Choosing appearance over appropriateness.

**Solution:** Match plants to your conditions (light, climate) and skill level. Pretty plants in wrong conditions become ugly dead plants fast. Start with plants that thrive in your space, then expand to favorites once you have skills.

### Mistake 3: Following Watering Schedules Instead of Checking Soil

"Water every day" or "water twice a week" advice fails because it ignores changing conditions. Beginners water on autopilot and kill plants through kindness.

**Solution:** The finger test every single time before watering. Stick your finger in soil. Water if dry, skip if moist. This simple habit prevents 70% of problems.

### Mistake 4: Expecting Instant Results

Gardening works on plant time, not human time. Impatience makes beginners give up before seeing results.

**Solution:** Choose some fast-growing plants (methi, coriander, radish) for quick wins while slower plants (tomatoes, flowers) establish. Celebrate small milestones—first sprout, first true leaf, first bud, first harvest.

### Mistake 5: Not Observing Before Buying

Purchasing plants, then realizing you don't have the right light or space for them. Buying shade plants for a sunny balcony or sun lovers for a dim windowsill.

**Solution:** Complete the 3-day sunlight observation BEFORE buying anything. Match plant requirements to your actual conditions, not to wishful thinking.

### Mistake 6: Using Construction Soil or Wrong Containers

Filling pots with random soil from construction sites (compacted clay), or using containers without drainage holes. Plants can't root in compacted soil and rot in waterlogged containers.

**Solution:** Buy or mix proper potting medium. Drill drainage holes in any recycled container. This foundation prevents endless problems.

### Mistake 7: Giving Up After the First Failure

Killing one plant and concluding "I don't have a green thumb." Gardening skill is learned, not genetic.

**Solution:** Every dead plant teaches something. Ask "What killed it?" Adjust one variable (location, watering frequency, soil). Try again. Improvement happens through iteration.

☐ Add first compost feeding around day 20-21

☐ Adjust plant locations if any show stress from too much/too little light

### Do Week Four (Days 23-30)

☐ Continue daily observation and watering routine

☐ Harvest first leaves/herbs if ready (8-10 mature leaves present)

☐ Take photos of your plants—compare to Day 1 photos

☐ Note any problems encountered and solutions that worked

☐ Decide whether to expand (add 2-3 more pots) or consolidate current learning

☐ Celebrate your success—one month of consistent gardening!
